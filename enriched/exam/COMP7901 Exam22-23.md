## QUESTION 1 (30 Marks)

Nick, a professor of surgery at Pokfulam University (PU), has come up with a new and ingenious method ("the Method") for heart surgery which minimises the risks of fatal incidents on the operating table and avoids most of the complications that often occurred after operation. To facilitate operations based on the Method, Nick has also designed a new and special device ("the Device") to be used by the surgeon during heart operation surgery. Nick has just filed a standard patent application ("the Application") in Hong Kong with a process claim for the Method and a product claim for the Device.

**(a) Are Nick's claims in the Application excluded from patentability under the Patents Ordinance? Explain. (10 marks)**

### Suggested answer (a)

- **Method (process claim):** A method of treatment of the human body by surgery is generally excluded from patentability. A process claim framed as “a method of heart surgery” is therefore very likely **excluded**.
- **Device (product claim):** A surgical **device** is not a method of medical treatment; it is a product capable of industrial application and is generally **not excluded** merely because it is used in surgery. Usual requirements (novelty, inventive step, sufficiency, clarity/support) still apply.

**(b) Regardless of your answer to (a) above, assume that the Application succeeds eventually and a standard patent is granted. Nick's employment contract with PU states that his main duties are teaching and conducting research in brain surgery, and mentions nothing about patents. Who is entitled to the standard patent? Explain. (10 Marks)**

### Suggested answer (b)

- Starting point: the patent belongs to the **inventor** unless the employee-invention rules vest it in the employer.
- Under the Patents Ordinance employee-invention rule (course of duties / special obligation), PU is entitled if:
  - the invention was made in the course of Nick’s normal or specifically assigned duties **and** an invention could reasonably be expected to result; or
  - Nick’s duties and responsibilities imposed a **special obligation** to further PU’s interests.
- On these facts, Nick is employed to conduct **surgical research** (even if “brain surgery” is specified). Developing a new surgical method/device is a classic research output that could reasonably be expected from such duties, likely using PU’s facilities.
- **Likely conclusion:** PU has a strong claim to be entitled to the patent (at least for inventions arising from Nick’s research duties), unless Nick can show the work was clearly outside his duties and not reasonably expected.

**(c) The Device in Nick's Application consists of 3 essential components, referred to as C1, C2 and C3. Prior to the Application, both C1 and C2 had already been used in a similar device ("Device0") designed for surgeons to perform brain operations. While the Application was still being processed, an article was published in a surgery journal showing that C3 was not needed for the Device. Would this new finding affect Nick's Application (and if so, how)? Explain. (10 Marks)**

### Suggested answer (c)

- Patentability is assessed against prior art **as at the filing/priority date**. A later journal article is generally **not** prior art for novelty/inventive step (unless it evidences what was already publicly known before filing).
- However, the new finding matters in practice because:
  - If C1+C2 were already known in Device0, and C3 turns out to contribute no real technical effect, the “difference” over Device0 may be attacked as **obvious** (or as lacking a meaningful inventive contribution) when the application is examined or later challenged.
  - Nick may try to **amend** to remove C3, but then the claim may become anticipated/obvious over Device0 (and must also stay within the original disclosure).
- **Net effect:** the later article does not automatically destroy novelty, but it can significantly undermine **inventive step / validity** arguments for a claim whose only distinguishing feature is C3.

## QUESTION 2 (30 Marks)

"MoneyHeist" is a computer game developed by HKGames Limited. MoneyHeist's object code is placed on HKGames' website for free download on condition that the game is used for private and domestic purposes. The game features a team of robbers inside a central bank, each with a different skill. The player is required to coordinate the team to prevent the police from entering the bank while money is being printed. The player wins the game when the money printed is successfully delivered to a destination outside the bank. A score will then be added to the player's account based on the number of surviving robbers and the sum of money delivered to the destination.

David is a fan of MoneyHeist. Purely out of curiosity, he has decompiled MoneyHeist's object code to study the resultant assembly code. After much trial and error, he was able to locate the module of object code responsible for the robbers' skills and replace it with a completely new module of object code generated by himself. The result was a team of robbers with more sophisticated skills rendering the game much more exciting. David named this enhanced game "MoneyChase". A month later, David decided to share MoneyChase with others by placing its object code on his website for free download. To date, more than 5,000 users have downloaded MoneyChase.

**(a) Does David have any civil liabilities under the Copyright Ordinance? What about criminal liabilities? Explain. (12 Marks)**

### Suggested answer (a)

- **Civil liability:**
  - A computer program is protected as a literary work; decompiling typically involves making copies/adaptations (and “translation” between code forms is treated as an adaptation).
  - David’s replacement module likely creates an adapted/derivative version of MoneyHeist (at minimum he reproduces substantial parts when he decompiles and when he distributes the combined program).
  - Uploading MoneyChase for public download infringes the owner’s restricted acts (copying and making available/communicating to the public).
  - The “private and domestic purposes” term is a licence condition; David’s acts go beyond the licence, strengthening infringement and also creating potential contractual breach.
- **Criminal liability:**
  - Criminal offences generally focus on commercial dealing / business contexts or distribution to an extent prejudicial to the copyright owner.
  - Even if David distributed for free, 5,000 downloads can be argued to be **prejudicial**; whether the precise statutory criminal thresholds are met depends on the offence charged and proof of knowledge/extent.

He then sent MoneyDaze's object code to a factory in Thailand instructing it to make mass production of CD copies of the object code and courier the CDs back to him. What about criminal liabilities? Explain. (18 Marks)**

- **Civil liability:** Edward reproduced a substantial part of MoneyHeist in MoneyDaze and then caused mass copying and issued copies to the public (sale in Hong Kong). Those are classic infringements of the restricted acts (copying, issuing copies to the public, etc.). Instructing an overseas factory does not avoid liability when the infringing copies are imported and commercially dealt with in Hong Kong.
- **Criminal liability:** Mass production for sale and selling CDs through retailers is squarely within the typical **business/commercial** infringement offences (making, importing, possessing for sale, selling/distributing infringing copies). Edward’s knowledge is strongly inferable because he incorporated MoneyHeist code and organised manufacture and sales.

## QUESTION 3 (30 Marks)

**(a) Linda, who obtained a PhD degree from Bonham University (BU) 5 years ago, was recently stripped of the degree by BU on the ground that her PhD thesis has plagiarised an earlier thesis kept in the library of a university in the US. The decision was made a week ago after a confidential hearing before BU's special committee on academic matters ("the Committee"). Linda attended the hearing and, after answering questions raised by members of the Committee, was requested to leave the room while the Committee deliberated on their decision. The entire hearing was audio recorded. BU's regulations allow a student to appeal against the decision of the Committee. Linda has just asked BU for a copy of the audio recording. Must BU comply under the Personal Data (Privacy) Ordinance, and if so, how? Explain. (15 Marks)**

### Suggested answer (a)

- The audio recording likely contains Linda’s **personal data** (her voice, statements, and information about her). BU is a data user holding that data.
- Linda can make a **data access request** for her personal data. BU must comply within the statutory time limit **unless** a refusal ground applies.
- Key limitation: the recording also contains personal data of **other data subjects** (committee members, witnesses, etc.). BU must refuse if it cannot comply without disclosing another data subject’s personal data, **unless** it can comply by **redaction/omission** (e.g., providing a transcript with identifiers removed, or an edited audio where feasible).
- The PDPO generally requires providing the requested personal data in an **intelligible form**, not necessarily in the exact medium requested. So BU may comply by providing a **transcript** of the parts that are Linda’s personal data, with appropriate redactions, and by explaining any withheld portions and the reasons.

**(b) Keith, the principal of a conservative secondary school, has undergone a divorce proceeding with his wife, Maggie. By an order of the court, Keith was to move out of the matrimonial home before a certain date but he has failed to do so. Furious that Keith has not complied with the court order, Maggie has posted a notice outside their matrimonial home demanding Keith to move out. The notice contains information relating to the divorce proceeding and mentioning Keith's adultery as the ground of divorce. The notice has been seen by many residents in the neighbourhood, including some students at Keith's school. Can Keith take any action against Maggie under the Personal Data (Privacy) Ordinance? Does Maggie have any defence? Explain. (15 Marks)**

### Suggested answer (b)

- The notice discloses information “relating to” Keith (divorce, alleged adultery) and can identify him, so it is likely **personal data**.
- Potential PDPO angles:
  - **Doxxing offences** (if made without consent with intent/recklessness to cause specified harm, and harm is caused): posting address/location-linked personal data to expose someone can fall within the anti-doxxing provisions depending on proof of intent/recklessness and resulting harm.
  - **Complaint/enforcement**: Keith can complain to the Privacy Commissioner; enforcement typically targets contraventions of the Data Protection Principles by a “data user”.
  - **Compensation**: Keith may sue for compensation only if he proves a contravention and resulting damage (including emotional distress).
- **Defences/exemptions:** Maggie may argue the **domestic/personal affairs** exemption, or that the disclosure was connected with **legal proceedings**/enforcement of rights. But posting a public notice with sensitive allegations may be hard to justify as “necessary” for those purposes and may be viewed as excessive.
