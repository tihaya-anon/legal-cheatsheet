## QUESTION 1. (30 Marks)

"AI-Dog", a robotic dog produced by UniqToys Limited that responds to spoken Cantonese, is a huge success in Hong Kong. AI-Dog's ability to understand and respond to spoken Cantonese is attributed to an internal chip, "UniqX", which implements the "Messi Method" for recognizing human speech invented by Messi, UniqToys' chief engineer. In 2022, UniqToys was granted a standard patent in Hong Kong in respect of UniqX and the Messi Method. The two main claims in the patent are, in essence:

(1) An intelligent method (Messi Method) for recognizing human speech; and

(2) An intelligent chip (UniqX) for recognizing human speech in Cantonese based on the Messi Method. The description of UniqX indicates that it consists of 3 essential elements.

Since January 2024, a new robotic dog known as "Genius", manufactured by DreamToys Limited, has appeared in the Hong Kong market. Targeted at the global market, Genius responds to human speech in English. Curious as to how it works, Messi bought a Genius and conducted a thorough analysis. He discovered that Genius contains a chip known as "DreamY" which implements the Messi Method but is adapted to recognise spoken English. Further examination revealed that DreamY comprises 2 of the 3 essential elements of UniqX, plus 1 other essential element.

### (a) (15 marks)

Are there grounds for UniqToys to bring a legal action against DreamToys (and if so, in respect of what subject matter) under the Patents Ordinance? Explain.

### Suggested answer (1a)

- **Start with claim construction**: whether claim (1) is limited to Cantonese or covers “human speech” generally is decisive.
- **Method claim (Messi Method):**
  - If not limited to Cantonese, implementing the same core method (even adapted to English) is likely **use of the patented process** in Hong Kong → infringement.
  - If limited to Cantonese recognition, DreamToys can argue non-infringement (English recognition falls outside).
- **Chip claim (UniqX with 3 essential elements):**
  - DreamY has only **2 of the 3 essential elements** plus a different element → strong argument of **no literal infringement**.
  - UniqToys may argue **equivalents** (variant analysis), but that is fact-sensitive and weaker where an “essential element” is missing.
- **Overall**: strongest case is usually on the **method claim** (subject to how it is drafted).

## QUESTION 2. (30 Marks)

### (a) (15 Marks)

Alex and Betty had a short relationship which ended last month. Enraged by the breakup, Alex downloaded information from Betty's profile on a professionals' website including her photos, name, employer, and her position. He then posted all such information on several social media platforms together with Betty's residential address and her private and office telephone numbers. Alex also impersonated Betty and opened accounts in her name on the said platforms. From these accounts he posted messages inviting men to stop by Betty's residential address where she is living with her parents. Many strangers have since contacted Betty and some unknown men have shown up at her door. What are Alex's liabilities, if any, under the Personal Data (Privacy) Ordinance? Explain.

### Suggested answer (2a)

- The posted items (photos, name, employer/position, home address, phone numbers) are **personal data** identifying Betty.
- The PDPO’s main exposure here is the **anti-doxxing offences**: disclosure without consent with intent/recklessness to cause “specified harm” (harassment/intimidation/psychological harm), and potentially the aggravated offence if harm is actually caused (strangers showing up is strong evidence of harm).
- Alex is also likely a “data user” controlling the disclosure; public posting for retaliation is unlikely to be protected by the **domestic/personal affairs** exemption.
- Outcomes: criminal investigation/prosecution by/with the Privacy Commissioner; Betty can also complain and may seek compensation if she proves contravention and resulting damage (including emotional distress).

### (b) (15 Marks)

Charles has suffered heavy loss in the stock market in the past few months. He wanted to find out whether his brokerage company, Bestrade Limited, was at fault in handling his trading instructions on telephone. He made a request to Bestrade for obtaining: (1) telephone conversation records between him and Bestrade's stock hotline on various dates; (2) the identity of each Bestrade staff he spoke to on those dates; (3) copies of Bestrade's guidelines on handling customers' stock accounts and rules on calculating account balance after trading; and (4) copies of all related reports on his stock trading account submitted by Bestrade as confidential documents to the government. Advise Bestrade if it should comply with Charles' request and, if so, how? Explain.

### Suggested answer (2b)

- A data access request entitles Charles to **his personal data**, not to general corporate materials.
- **(1) Telephone conversation records:** contain Charles’ personal data → Bestrade should generally **comply** (recordings or intelligible transcripts), subject to any specific statutory exemption and reasonable fee.
- **(2) Identity of staff:** staff identities are also **personal data of the staff**. Bestrade must refuse if it cannot comply without disclosing another data subject’s personal data, unless it can comply by **redaction/omission** (e.g., provide staff IDs rather than names where appropriate).
- **(3) Internal guidelines/rules:** not Charles’ personal data → Bestrade may **refuse** under the PDPO (though it can disclose voluntarily).
- **(4) “Confidential reports to government”:** if they contain Charles’ personal data, Bestrade should comply **to that extent**, but may refuse/withhold parts if prohibited by another ordinance or if a PDPO exemption applies; provide **redacted** copies and reasons.

## QUESTION 3. (30 Marks)

LetFlix Limited is an exclusive licensee of MegaFilms Limited for using streaming (a method of viewing video or audio content online without having to download the media files) to deliver movies produced by MegaFilms to LetFlix's paid subscribers online. LetFlix's terms of subscription clearly states that its service is only for the subscriber's personal and non-commercial use.

NuTube Limited is an online entertainment provider that streams movies to its subscribers free of charge. Apart from focusing on Cantonese films, NuTube's movies all come with advertisements and cannot be downloaded. NuTube's terms of subscription also states that its free service is only for the subscriber's personal and non-commercial use.

### (a) (15 Marks)

Recently, LetFlix discovered that some of its Cantonese films ("the Films") were found in NuTube's service. At the time of their release, the Films were without subtitles but all contained subtitles in English ("Subtitled Films") at the time of discovery. NuTube has publicly said that the English subtitles were written by its staff with a view to introducing Hong Kong's culture to the world. Does NuTube have any liabilities under the Copyright Ordinance? Explain.

### Suggested answer (3a)

- Streaming the Films to the public without licence typically infringes restricted acts (including making available/communicating to the public and the necessary copying on servers).
- LetFlix, as **exclusive licensee** for streaming, can generally sue within the scope of its exclusive rights.
- Adding English subtitles does not legitimise the use:
  - Subtitles often involve copying/translation of protected dialogue/script and creating a derivative element.
  - Even if NuTube’s staff own copyright in the subtitle text, NuTube still needs permission to use the underlying Films.
- “Introducing culture” is not a statutory defence; fair dealing is unlikely given the substitute streaming service context and ad-supported operation.
